What changed

Two rule changes that shape the decision

Solar in California isn't what it was a few years ago. Two recent shifts are the reason we're approaching this the way we are.

Rule change 01

Exporting solar barely pays now

Until 2023, the utility credited you the full retail rate for solar you sent back. Under the current rules (NEM 3.0), it pays only its "avoided cost" — about $0.05–0.08/kWh — while you still pay $0.30–0.55+ to buy power back.

So the value now is in using your own solar as you make it and storing the rest. That's why the battery is the centerpiece here, not just the panels.

Rule change 02

The 30% federal credit is gone

The credit for buying a solar system expired December 31, 2025. A large, professionally installed system used to lean on that 30% to pencil out.

Without it, an expensive whole-home install is much harder to justify — which points us toward a smaller system we build and own ourselves.

The approach

The plan, in plain terms

Put a modest solar array and a battery behind the loads that matter, charge the battery on daytime sun, and run those loads — especially through the 4–9 PM peak — from stored power instead of the grid. Start small, prove it out, and add panels and batteries over time.

The system is "islanded": it powers its own loads and never pushes power back to the grid or ties into the main electrical panel. That keeps it entirely outside the utility's process — no permit, no interconnection application, no waiting.

Two deliberate choices

Where we save, and where we don't

Save here · Panels

Used panels are fine

Because the system is off-grid, we aren't bound by the utility inspection rules that normally require new, labeled panels. We can buy a tested used lot for a fraction of new — the panels are the safe place to economize.

Don't cut corners · Battery

Certified batteries only

We use LiFePO4 chemistry, which resists the thermal-runaway failures associated with other lithium batteries, and we require UL 9540A testing — the standard that verifies a single cell fault won't spread into a fire.
